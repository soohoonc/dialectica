# Global Missionary Era

The Global Missionary Era (c. 1500-1950) marks the period when organized religious missions became deeply entangled with empire, print culture, schooling, translation, and legal administration across continents. In this graph, it is less about a single doctrine than a historical infrastructure of movement: people, texts, institutions, and conversion projects.

## Why This Period Matters

Missionary expansion reshaped political authority, education systems, and cultural translation. It also generated counter-movements, reform projects, and later anti-colonial critiques.

## Institutional Engines

- Confessional conflicts in Europe (see [[protestant-reformation]] and [[counter-reformation-era]]) structured missionary competition.
- New publishing and school networks carried doctrine, law, and social norms through colonial and commercial routes.
- Mission practice often overlapped with governance, diplomacy, and ethnographic knowledge production.

## Encounter Zones

Key sites in this graph include [[rome]], [[london]], [[beijing]], [[delhi]], [[cairo]], and [[tenochtitlan]]. These spaces capture different missionary logics: court diplomacy, urban schooling, translation projects, and coercive religious restructuring.

## Legacy

By the mid-20th century, missionary institutions had helped shape modern debates on rights, education, sovereignty, and postcolonial identity. This period feeds directly into [[postcolonial-period]].
