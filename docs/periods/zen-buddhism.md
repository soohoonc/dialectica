# Zen Buddhism

**Time Period**: 520 - present

## Overview

The Buddhist tradition emphasizing direct experiential realization through meditation, transmitted from China to Japan and beyond

## Key Ideas

### [[individuals/dogen|Dogen]]

- **Just Sitting (Shikantaza)**: The Zen practice of objectless meditation without seeking enlightenment, understanding sitting itself as the expression of Buddha-nature
  - Tags: #Zen, #Meditation, #BuddhaNature
- **Being-Time (Uji)**: The philosophical teaching that being and time are not separate, with each moment expressing the complete Buddha-dharma
  - Tags: #Zen, #Time, #Existence

## Intellectual Networks

This tradition developed a unique approach to Buddhist enlightenment emphasizing direct pointing to the mind, non-dependence on words and letters, and the realization of one's inherent Buddha-nature.

### Most Influential Ideas

- Direct transmission from mind to mind outside verbal teachings
- The identity of practice and enlightenment (shusho-itto)
- Non-dualistic understanding of Buddha-nature and ordinary mind
- Integration of daily activities with spiritual practice

## Timeline

- **c. 520 CE**: Bodhidharma traditionally brings Zen from India to China
- **638-713**: Huineng, the Sixth Patriarch, establishes Southern School
- **840-865**: Chinese persecution of Buddhism spreads Zen to Japan
- **1191**: Eisai introduces Rinzai Zen to Japan
- **1227**: [[individuals/dogen|Dogen]] introduces Soto Zen to Japan
- **1200-1500**: Golden age of Japanese Zen monastery culture
- **1868-present**: Meiji restoration and modern transmission to the West

## Context

Zen Buddhism emerged in China as Chan during the Tang Dynasty (618-907), representing a distinctively Chinese interpretation of Indian Mahayana Buddhism influenced by Daoist naturalism. The tradition flourished during periods of cultural synthesis and creative adaptation.

In Japan, Zen developed alongside samurai culture, influencing arts like tea ceremony, flower arrangement, and martial arts. The tradition adapted to different cultural contexts while maintaining its essential emphasis on direct experience and practical wisdom.

## Legacy

Zen Buddhism profoundly influenced culture and philosophy:

- **[[periods/japanese-philosophy|Japanese Philosophy]]**: Influenced the Kyoto School and modern Japanese thought
- **Arts and Aesthetics**: Shaped distinctive approaches to poetry, painting, architecture, and garden design
- **Western Psychology**: Influenced humanistic and transpersonal psychology
- **[[periods/continental-philosophy|Continental Philosophy]]**: Engaged with by philosophers like Heidegger and the Kyoto School
- **Mindfulness Movement**: Contemporary secular adaptations of Zen meditation practices

The tradition's emphasis on non-conceptual awareness, the integration of wisdom and compassion, and the realization of interdependence continues to offer valuable resources for addressing contemporary questions about consciousness, ethics, and the relationship between philosophy and spiritual practice.
