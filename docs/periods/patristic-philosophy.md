# Patristic Philosophy

**Time Period**: 100 CE - 800 CE

## Overview

Early Christian philosophers integrating Greek philosophical methods with Christian doctrine

## Key Ideas

### [[individuals/augustine|Augustine]]

- **Augustine's Theory of Time**: Exploration of the nature of time as a distension of the mind, connecting memory, attention, and expectation
  - Tags: #Metaphysics, #Time, #Psychology
- **Divine Illumination**: The theory that human knowledge of eternal truths depends on divine illumination rather than empirical experience
  - Tags: #Epistemology, #Theology

### [[individuals/anselm-of-canterbury|Anselm of Canterbury]]

- **Ontological Argument**: The a priori argument for God's existence based on the concept of God as that than which nothing greater can be conceived
  - Tags: #Theology, #Logic, #Ontology

## Intellectual Networks

This period synthesized Greek philosophical traditions with Christian revelation, creating new approaches to metaphysics, epistemology, and ethics within a theological framework.

### Most Influential Ideas

- Augustine's integration of Platonic philosophy with Christian doctrine
- The development of natural theology and philosophical arguments for God's existence
- Christian anthropology and the concept of original sin

## Timeline

- **c. 100-165 CE**: Justin Martyr attempts first systematic Christian philosophy
- **c. 185-254 CE**: Origen develops Christian Platonism in Alexandria
- **354-430 CE**: [[individuals/augustine|Augustine]] synthesizes Christianity and Platonism
- **c. 480-524 CE**: Boethius translates and comments on Aristotelian logic
- **c. 515-615 CE**: Pseudo-Dionysius develops Christian Neoplatonism
- **c. 580-662 CE**: Maximus the Confessor advances Eastern Christian theology
- **c. 675-749 CE**: John of Damascus systematizes Eastern Orthodox doctrine
- **c. 810-877 CE**: John Scottus Eriugena introduces Dionysian thought to the West

## Context

The Patristic period emerged as Christianity transformed from a persecuted sect to the official religion of the Roman Empire. Church Fathers faced the challenge of articulating Christian beliefs in philosophical terms that could compete with sophisticated pagan philosophy while maintaining doctrinal orthodoxy.

The period witnessed the decline of classical paganism, the barbarian invasions, the fall of the Western Roman Empire, and the preservation of classical learning in monasteries. Major theological controversies (Arianism, Pelagianism, Monophysitism) required philosophical precision in formulating orthodox doctrine.

## Legacy

Patristic philosophy established lasting foundations for Western Christian thought:

- **[[periods/medieval-philosophy|Medieval Philosophy]]**: Provided the framework for Scholastic theology and philosophy
- **Philosophical Theology**: Established the discipline of using reason to understand faith
- **Christian Platonism**: Created an influential synthesis of Platonic metaphysics and Christian doctrine
- **Moral Psychology**: Augustine's analysis of will, desire, and moral conflict influenced later ethics
- **Political Philosophy**: Augustine's "City of God" established Christian political theory

The period's integration of faith and reason, emphasis on interiority and personal experience, and development of sophisticated theological arguments profoundly shaped both [[periods/medieval-philosophy|medieval]] and [[periods/modern-philosophy|modern]] philosophy, while its influence extends to contemporary Christian philosophy and theology.
